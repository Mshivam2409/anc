RISE With The Seniors

Beast Named Research..

June 30, 2019

In the next article of the blog series Saakaar Bhatnagar, an undergraduate student in the Department of Aerospace Engineering from the Y15 batch shares his experience with application procedure to graduate schools in the USA before he finally got admitted to Stanford University for his graduate studies.

Stanford University is one of the world’s leading research universities situated in California’s Bay area. It was ranked 2nd in QS World University rankings 2019 with it's Aerospace Engineering program being one of the best in the world

I stepped into the IIT Kanpur campus on 21st July 2015, with all the fears and doubts one associates with living away from home for the first time, certain of only one thing: that for the next four years, I would study Aerospace Engineering. A fascination, that started during my JEE days, with the complexity of flying objects put me on the path I am on today, (almost) a graduate student in the US looking to delve into one of the many subfields of Aerospace Engineering.

Starting out, I had some apprehensions being an AE student. Being bombarded with views like “abbe job nahi lagti”, “coding seekh lena”, “core placement zero hai” - made me wonder what the hell I cleared JEE for, certainly not for this negative vibe. However, I had also heard that the AE department at IITK has an excellent international standing, with some leading professors in their respective subfields doing cutting edge research. Hence, I decided that this beast called research should be given a try, and one and a half years later, I found myself applying for graduate school, and was lucky enough to get admitted to some of my top school choices. This article is mostly about the process of application, and I skip my experience of doing research work at IITK and at the University of Michigan, Ann Arbor, where I was a summer research intern. I only applied to schools in the United States, and the following information would be helpful to you if you are considering graduate school in the US. Also, my applications were a mixture of MS and PhD applications, based on a number of factors, including the facilities the university had for my field of interest, the level of difficulty to get into the PhD program for that university, amongst others. This was keeping in mind that my goal was to go into research.

The application process for graduate school abroad consists of a long checklist to finish, along with some daunting decisions to be made on the way. In order to make the process easier, it helps to have an idea of what universities look for when judging applications. Post graduate applications are mostly critically judged on the apparent (from the application) capability of the candidate to conduct research over the next few years, much more so for PhD applications than MS applications. They use several metrics to judge this, and putting together all they want forms a majority of the journey of applying to grad school.

Starting in early August, I discovered I had a lot to accomplish in the next few months! I had to take the GRE and the TOEFL, put together all the documents required for filling out the applications, write the dreaded SOPs, obtain the letters of recommendation, scout out the universities where my research interests were good fits, fill out the application forms, all the while managing academics and under-graduate project work. So I divided my application process into two halves.

The first half, from the start of August to the end of September, was focused on the GRE and TOEFL and preparing all required documents like CV, passport and the like. Then, like some others, I was nervous about the GRE. Although I had a pretty strong background in English, speaking mostly English at home as I grew up, the last time I read a non-academic English text was seven years ago, and this showed in my verbal practice test scores. So I put in some focused effort on the verbal sections over the next two months, which of course included vocabulary building as well. A couple of points about the GRE for prospective test takers: first, the time needed to prepare for the GRE is a subjective matter, and for engineering students, it depends mostly on your confidence in your facility with English. I managed in two months (they were semester months with little free time), but the best way to judge for yourself is to take a practice test (legitimately, in one go, under near identical test conditions without any fraud) and see where you stand. Secondly, the importance of the GRE is gradually diminishing, with universities mostly using it as a filtration criteria. It never hurts to have your GRE score as high as possible, but usually a 325-330 with an AWA of 4.5 is more than enough for any target university you may have. As for the TOEFL, I only prepared for two-three days using YouTube, but it again depends on your facility with the usage of English in everyday situations.

After getting satisfactory scores on the tests, and after spending hours getting the relevant documents ready, I was in early October. It was time to write the SOPs. One essentially has up to two pages to sell himself/herself, appearing confident but not proud, appearing open to different ideas the culture of the university has to offer while also projecting a clear idea of what he/she would like to accomplish during his/her stay there, and maybe even after that. Naturally, the SOP for each university should be unique (to be effective), although one may follow similar trains of thought throughout the essay for all of them. It took a month of effort, and several drafts, modified with feedback from people from several different walks of life, to get the SOPs ready.

The final step was filling the massive application forms, and getting the letters of recommendation. Usually one is required to submit up to three LORs, though certain universities allow submission of up to six. Two of my LORs were from my research mentors, and the third from a course instructor, whose course I did well in. LORs were probably the most important part of my application, since I did not have a publication at the time of submitting my application forms. It is important to ask for your LORs at least a month before the deadlines for applications, which usually begin in December. Professors are extremely busy people, and it is in the applicant’s interest to make the process of writing the letters as easy as possible for them, so that they get enough time to ponder on and write strong letters. It also always helps to, while asking for the letters, submit a summary of coursework and project-work done with them. While on internship, towards the end of the internship I very carefully documented everything I did during the internship, and the goals that were met. It gave my recommenders more material and jogged their memory about what I did with them. As a student, one does not usually control this part of the application, both the content of the letters and when they are submitted. Some of my letters were submitted uncomfortably close to the deadline, which made me rather queasy as a stickler for deadlines!

And I was done! A semester long journey ended (thankfully), and it was time to cool my heels and wait for the results. The letters came in starting January all the way to April, and the wait during that period was, admittedly, pretty heart wrenching. I got my fair share of admits and rejects, from both MS and PhD applications, and was fortunate enough to be admitted to some of my top choices. Making the final choice between schools is always a phenomenally difficult decision, and was no different for me. In the end, it is just about making the most informed, objective and guided choice. As a result, I find myself heading to Stanford University to pursue my graduate studies.

As I wrote this article, the consequentiality of the journey that I have undertaken, and the enormity of what is still to come, struck me. Although this blog only covered my journey of the application process,the truth is that everything one does from day one at IIT Kanpur, plays a role in where one will eventually go, more so for graduate school than anything else. When I entered IITK, I was already pretty motivated and willing to go any distance to do well at whatever I did. What I realize at the end of my journey, however, is that more than my individual determination, it was the brilliance and discipline of the people around me that drove me the most. I count myself as very fortunate to have had people to guide me, motivate me and give me chances when I needed them the most, not just in academics but in all aspects of my life.

To conclude, I would add that the world of research and graduate school I have seen so far, is different from the classical rat race we in India are so used to running: there is no blueprint for how things are supposed to happen, and no set path to guarantee victory or success. Both enjoyment and success indeed depend on how much you enjoy wrestling with problems, obstacles and challenges on a daily basis!

- Saakaar Bhatnagar
