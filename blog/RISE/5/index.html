RISE With The Seniors

A Master's Application Guide

July 14, 2019

In the fifth article of the blog series - RISE, Devansh Saraswat an undergraduate student in the Department of Materials Science Engineering from the Y15 batch pens down in depth information about the major components of graduate application in a detailed manner along with some of his enlightening tips, making it a must read for sure.
He will be heading to Cornell University this Fall semester for pursuing a Master's degree in MSE.

Cornell University is a private and statutory Ivy League research university in Ithaca, New York that has been ranked 14 in the QS World University Ranking 2019 and is one of the premium institutes of research.

One of the most stressful, time-consuming, and tedious period for any Masters or Ph.D. aspirant is the university application phase. Which college should I apply to? How should I shortlist the universities? How many colleges should I apply to? What college will I get into based on my profile? These are some of the several questions faced by any final year student. Through this blog, I wish to address the majority of these critical questions and share my experiences of applying to Mater’s programs in US universities.

Before starting with the details, I would like to introduce myself in a few lines. I have completed my B. Tech in Materials Science and Engineering from IIT Kanpur. Now I will be heading to Cornell University this Fall semester for pursuing a Master's degree in MSE.

The major components of a graduate application for a US university are as follows:

  • GRE and TOEFL scores
  • Your transcript (grades till the sixth semester)
  • Statement of Purpose
  • Letters of Recommendation
  • Online Graduate School Application

Now I will go into the details of these major components.

GRE and TOEFL
Any student going to the US for graduate studies needs to appear for the GRE and TOEFL irrespective of his/her discipline. I will describe these two exams in short here. GRE (Graduate Record Examination) General Test consists of three sections: 1) Analytical Writing, 2) Verbal Reasoning, and 3) Quantitative Reasoning. The analytical writing section consists of two essay-based questions. This section is graded out of 6. The verbal section tests your vocabulary and in-depth understanding of the English language. The quantitative section evaluates your mental ability and consists of basic mathematics questions that any engineering student can easily solve. Both these sections are worth 170 points each.

TOEFL is a computer-based exam for testing your English skills. It has four sections: Reading, Listening, Speaking, and Writing. Each section is worth 30 points each. Each university sets a minimum cut off score for TOEFL. Most universities have their cut-offs around 80-90, very few have as high as 105 for engineering students.

I began preparing for GRE and TOEFL after I returned to IITK after completing my research internship. I think a one-month preparation is adequate for getting a good score. At this moment, most of the readers would have this question in their mind as to what is a ‘good score'. In my opinion, any score above 320 out of 340 is a good score in GRE. One thing to keep in mind is that GRE scores are not the only criteria for selection; you are evaluated based on your GRE score, GPA, SOP, and LORs. All of the above matter and having a high score/evaluation in any one of these does not mean automatic/ guaranteed selection. So even if you have a lower score, you can still get selected if you perform well on the other criteria.

There are numerous online resources that you can use for studying for GRE. For instance, I used the MAGOOSH GRE FLASHCARDS app for improving my vocabulary, which helped me to prepare for the Verbal Reasoning section. You don't need to study for the quantitative reasoning section. The questions are very trivial for an IIT student. After you take an appointment for your GRE exam on the ETS website, you will get access to two free official GRE practice tests. Additionally, there are other free GRE practice tests offered by Manhattan Prep, KAPLAN, etc.

I did not spend much time preparing for TOEFL. If you are studying for GRE, then there is no need to study more for TOEFL. The difficulty level of TOEFL is much lower than that of GRE. I practiced only for the speaking and listening section using some YouTube videos. No need to prepare for other sections.

Some tips which I would like to share based on my experiences of GRE and TOEFL:

  • Appear for at least 3-4 practice tests before the actual GRE. If you don't perform well in these practice tests, don't get disheartened. You will score about 4-5 more points in the actual exam if you have studied hard.
  • Some people tend to delay taking an appointment for GRE and TOEFL because they think that their vocabulary is not up to the mark, and they need to study a lot more. In my opinion, one-month preparation is more than enough for getting a good score. Study the whole of August and appear for GRE and TOEFL in the first week of September. You can schedule your GRE on a Saturday and TOEFL on a Sunday. This will also save a lot of your time, especially before the Mid Sem Exams.
  • During the TOEFL exam, it can get very noisy in the examination room during the speaking section. Although you will be wearing headphones, you will hear a lot of noise. One way to deal with this is to enter the examination room at last. If you adopt this strategy, it will be much easier to concentrate because by the time your speaking section will start, most of the others would have moved onto their writing section, and the room would be relatively quiet. I adopted this strategy and benefitted from it.
  • While taking practice tests, never skip the analytical writing section. This will help you to get accustomed to writing essays in a small time frame.


Selecting Universities
After appearing for GRE and TOEFL, I began shortlisting the universities. First, I made a list of universities (based on their rankings) along with their application deadlines. Then I browsed through the websites of each of these universities to learn about the research in my area of interest and looked at the profiles of the professors. After this, I made a final list of 12 universities out of which four were in the top 10, four in 10-25 and four were in a 25-50 range of the rankings.

Another important point that deters people from applying is their CPI. I would say that anyone who has CPI > 7.5 should apply. You can make up for your CPI by performing well on other parameters such as GRE, SOP, and LORs. Having a high CPI is not a guarantee of getting selected in top universities. As mentioned before, your application is evaluated on a lot of parameters, hence no need to panic if you don't perform well on one parameter.

Another misconception which I had in my mind while applying was the concept of so-called ‘safe university' where I thought I would get selected based on my credentials. This misconception was cleared when I got admission offers from universities I did not expect and was rejected by those which I considered as ‘safe'. Hence, I would advise everyone to even apply to those universities which you think are beyond you and don't consider any university as a definite safe option.

Most of these universities have their deadlines in late November, early and mid-December and mid-January. Hence, you have the entire winter break to utilize for completing your application. Go through the FAQs of your graduate program. You can get various other tips for a successful application at a particular university by reading the FAQs.

Another deterrent for most Indian students is the lack of funding. If you are applying for a Ph.D., then you will certainly receive a scholarship from your university which will take care of your expenses. The problem of funding is faced by Masters' aspirants. Most universities don't provide scholarship to the Master's students. But if you have good credentials, you may receive a partial scholarship of around $ 1000-2500 per semester. Many universities allow their master's students to work as research assistants or teaching assistants. The stipend you receive for these assistantships is enough to take care of your tuition fees. Also, if you have good credentials, then you can apply for external fellowships. A list of external fellowships can be found on UCLA's website. I would say that you should look at all these aspects before deciding to pursue a Master's degree in the US.

Statement of Purpose
In my opinion, this is the most important component of the application. There is no set pattern or way of writing an SOP. It is a personal choice as to how you want to present yourself in a few thousand words. Here, I am going to present a few pointers which I had in my mind while writing.

  • Start with a kind of short background story of how you got interested in science during your childhood, what motivated you to take up engineering and your hard work in clearing JEE.
  • Then, describe your academic experiences, important internships, projects, relevant courses, and publications.
  • You may discuss your hobbies, any social work you have undertaken, your teaching experience (e.g., academic mentor), etc.
  • An important point which you should include is the answer to the question- Why do you want to study at that university? While answering this question, don’t simply write that you are applying because that university is the best in the world and studying there will change your life. Talk about their academic program, research facilities, significant publications by professors, etc.
  • Present your research interests, skills and work experiences in a manner that will show that the research being done by professors in that university resonates with your interests and you will be a handy person to have in their research group.
  • Always write different SOPs for each university. Though this takes up time but gives the impression that you have researched well before applying and are seriously interested in studying at that university.
  • Try to get your SOP checked by your close ones. These can be friends, seniors, professors, or family members.
  • Always think about the impact your every sentence can have on the members of the graduate admissions committee. Choose your words judiciously.
  • Don’t make your SOP too long. Reviewers have to review hundreds of applications and don’t have much time. Two pages of single-spaced lines are enough.


Letters of Recommendation
LoRs are another important part of the application. Most universities require three letters of recommendation; some require only two although you can submit more if you want. These LoRs can be written by professors, previous employers, etc. You may also take a letter from your project manager if you went for a company internship, for research interns, you can take one from your research internship supervisor. Most universities explicitly mention that you have to take at least one LoR from a professor. I took two LoRs from professors at IITK and one from my research internship supervisor.

For taking an LoR from someone, first, seek permission from him/her and fill in his/her email and other details in the online application form. Then you can send a recommendation request to your recommender through the online application portal. Keep sending timely reminders to the recommenders and make sure that they are aware of the deadlines. I would recommend you to take LoRs from those professors who know you personally and are familiar with your scholastic and academic credentials.

The rough timeline I followed was :

  • Prepared for GRE and TOEFL – August
  • Appeared for both these exams- First week of September
  • Shortlisted universities- after mid sems in September and October
  • Started writing SOPs, requesting professors for LORs- November
  • Completed application forms, uploaded transcripts, reviewed SOPs- December


In this blog, I have shared my own experiences. This should not be considered as a template for university applications. Feel free to choose your path.
